There is no doubt that our way of life has changed since the beginning of the pandemic. As we adjust to this new sense of “normalcy”, we must collectively as a community follow social distancing guidelines to combat the spread of COVID-19. I believe that businesses have largely been impacted by the pandemic, specifically ride sharing services.
‍
Uber allows users to get to where they need to go in a convenient manner. But ridership has decreased since the start of the pandemic. My goal is to extend a feature of Uber to kickstart the ride sharing business once again and help get users where they need to go while ensuring their safety as a rider.

problem statement

College students rely on Uber as a means of reliable transportation for commuting to work and going to outings with friends. The coronavirus pandemic has instilled concerns among Uber riders. College students need a comfortable and convenient way to get to their destinations while being ensured that they are riding in a safe and clean car.

research findings

When the coronavirus pandemic hit the US back in March which led to mandatory shelter-in-place in cities across the country, research done by SuperFly found that “in the span of just four weeks, Uber rides fell as much as 94% in the United States ”.

user research

First, I conducted a survey using google forms to collect some quantitative data such as ridership before the start of quarantine and after and was able to receive 34 survey respondents.

I found thatat least 52.9% of the respondents used a ride sharing service more than once a month before the pandemic and also that every one of the respondents has used a ride sharing service at least once in a given year.

But 73.5% of respondents have not used a ride sharing service since the start of the pandemic.

So what is the reason for this big decline in ridership?

In order to answer this question I conducted 30 minute interviews with two college students who were frequent Uber riders before the pandemic. From my user research I found that many users use Uber for when they are going out to hangout with friends and when they don’t want to worry about parking. Users relied on Uber for convenience.

From the surveys, 79.4% of respondents said that they have concerns about using ride sharing services during the pandemic . Many users 'main concerns are risk of transmission, either due to the unknown cleanliness of the car and/or unknown number of previous riders and how often the car was cleaned between passengers. 

“I would use Uber when I’m going out and don’t want to find parking. Honestly I think you do lower your risk of getting infected by taking a private rideshare as opposed to public transportation. And one of the most important things is that rideshare can just drop you off directly at the place you want to be!”
“Even if they say it’s sanitized, I haven’t seen Uber put out any standardized protocols for drivers to follow.”
“It’s easier and usually faster to go around using Uber than taking the Muni (this is San Francisco’s public transit system) everywhere because I have to switch busses like three times.”
“There are other people who use it. I can’t control the other people and I don’t know how often they clean it.”

Conducting the interviews revealed some of the main concerns that people have with using Uber during the pandemic. I learned that riders are mainly concerned with the cleanliness of the car as well as the driver’s health. They don’t know whether the previous passengers are showing symptoms of COVID-19 or have been in contact with someone who is positive. These are also concerns they have for the driver. Hence the choice of avoiding using Uber during the pandemic.

competitive analysis

After distinguishing the core findings, I took a look at some of Uber 's competitors to research on how other company 's are leveraging their designs to combat some of the COVID-19 concerns. By understanding the competitors, I can use that insight alongside the user research findings to tactically design a good solution.

These two competitors already include something that Uber doesn’t, which is gentle reminders to users for following CDC guidelines to help keep everyone, riders and drivers, safe. But a key thing that Uber and their competitors lack is some way to inform users that the environment they will be using is clean and safe. My goal is to bridge that gap.

design process

The design process is a very iterative process. Throughout the scope of this project I went through several rounds of ideating and prototyping after getting feedback and doing further research in between.

beginning stages

I started out by creating two different user flows as well as doing some UI sketches of those flows before moving on to creating the low fidelity prototypes. Creating two different flows ensures that I won 't be super fixated on one specific idea. (click to view image)

After brainstorming and creating these two flows and making rough sketches for the UI and content of the wireframes, I continued by making low fidelity prototypes , Prototype A and B (respectively) in order to have some users test them out and give me some feedback.

Prototype A focuses on an added feature, the daily symptom screener which will either allow or not allow users to request a ride for the day. If a user is cleared, they will be able to request a ride and if they are not cleared, they will be locked and will not be able to request a ride for that day. They can take the symptom test again the next day and do the whole process again. This added feature will keeps riders and drivers safe. Other riders will be ensured that any previous rider in the car has taken the symptom screener and passed it that day as well.

Prototype B includes an added feature where when a user selects their ride type, they will be presented with a screen that asks them select their ride preference, specifically what types of PPE material would they like to be presented with or offered during the ride. By selecting these preferences, users will only be presented with drivers whose cars have those supplies. Before confirming the ride, users will be able to see information of the driver such as a note that they have been cleared to drive that day and the time that they last cleaned their car. Users will be able to review this information and decide to confirm and be on their way with the rest of the normal Uber flow or if they aren’t content they can select “Find another driver” to search for another nearby driver that meets their preferences. 

next steps

Following the first set of low fidelity prototypes was a vigorous cycle of designing, testing, and iterating. Upon getting feedback from user testing, I would either do some further research or go back to the the drawing board and ideate once more to refine some of the ideas I had and then prototype to test them out with users again.

the final design

This final prototype includes the added feature of daily symptom screening. Screening people daily before their first ride can keep people who may be experiencing symptoms from riding and ensure riders that other people who may have been in the same car as them earlier in the day are also cleared to ride. 

As mentioned before, something that Uber and their competitors lack is some way to inform users that the environment they will be using is clean and safe. Upon research, I found that during an app update around May 2020, all Uber users would be shown an "In this together "message that lists out some of the initiatives and rules implemented to keep their riders/drivers safe. But it is no longer a popup and cannot be found anywhere in the app. I made the decision to add it as a persistent banner on the app 's home page . It will always be accessible and will function as reassurance mechanism that Uber is taking actions to keep their riders and drivers safe during the pandemic!

Reflection

This project has made me realize how the design process is a super iterative process. Ideas will always change and/or improve as more data surfaces and more insight is gathered from user testing. I learned the importance of receiving feedback as well as giving good feedback to my classmates as they work on their projects as well. One of the most challenging parts of this project was working alone. I wish I had a teammate or teammates to rely on and combine more brain power towards tackling and framing the problem statement. Overall, I 've learned more about the design process from this project because of the hands-on nature of it than any other theory based class. It was a great learning experience and I hope to improve from here on out!
